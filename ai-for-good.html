The Concept
01

What is
Generative Design?

Generative design is an AI-assisted design process where you define constraints — loads, materials, mass targets, manufacturing methods — and let algorithms explore thousands of possible geometries simultaneously to find optimal solutions a human designer wouldn't think to try.

Unlike traditional CAD where you design a shape and then analyze it, generative design inverts the workflow: you define the performance goals and the software generates the geometry. The result is often organic-looking structures that are lighter and stronger than conventionally designed parts.

Tools like Autodesk Fusion's Generative Design workspace and SolidWorks Topology Study make this accessible to students and engineers working on real hardware.

"The best part isn't that it finds a better answer — it's that it finds answers you'd never have thought to ask about." — Personal reflection on using generative design at UMD Loop
1
Define the Design Space Set preserved geometry (where bolts attach), obstacle geometry (what it must clear), and the load cases it will experience.
2
Set Goals & Constraints Specify target mass, safety factor, material, and manufacturing method (3-axis mill, casting, additive, etc.).
3
Generate & Explore The algorithm explores hundreds of geometries across the Pareto front — trading off mass vs. stiffness vs. manufacturability.
4
Validate & Refine Select a promising outcome, run FEA to validate, and clean up geometry for manufacturing or printing.
5
Manufacture Export to CAM or slicer. Many generative outputs are designed specifically for additive manufacturing.

Where I've
Used It

01
UMD Loop · Rover Chassis
Swerve Module Bracket Optimization

When redesigning the swerve propulsion system for the UMD Loop Mars rover, the wheel module mounting brackets were a bottleneck — they were heavy, overbuilt in some areas and underdeveloped in others, and hard to disassemble quickly in the field.

Using Fusion's Generative Design workspace, I set up load cases based on the rover's expected impact forces during off-road traversal, defined the bolt pattern as preserved geometry, and constrained the output to 3D-printable geometry with PETG as the base material. The resulting bracket geometry reduced mass by roughly 22% compared to the hand-designed version while maintaining the same safety factor under the worst-case load scenarios.

The organic structure was then cleaned up in SolidWorks, validated with a manual FEA run, and printed in-house on the team's FDM printer with carbon fiber reinforcement in key stress zones.

02
UMD Loop · Robotic Arm
Differential Wrist Housing

The 3-DOF differential wrist needed a housing that was compact enough to fit within the arm's reach envelope, stiff enough to maintain gear mesh accuracy under load, and light enough not to degrade the arm's payload capacity.

I used SolidWorks Topology Study to optimize the wrist housing walls and rib structure, feeding in torque loads from the three Neo 550 motors and axial loads from the bevel gear meshing forces. The optimizer carved out material from low-stress regions while thickening walls near the bearing seats and gear thrust faces.

The final housing came in 31% lighter than the initial solid extrusion design, while stiffness at the output flange actually improved due to the addition of algorithmically-placed diagonal ribs the original design was missing.

03
Coursework · ENME
Structural Optimization in Mechanics II

In Mechanics II, a design project required creating a load-bearing bracket under a strict mass budget. Rather than iterating by hand, I ran a topology optimization study in Fusion to generate a starting geometry, then used the result as the basis for a parametric CAD model that could be adjusted to meet exact manufacturing constraints.

The exercise revealed an important limitation of generative tools: the outputs require engineering judgment to interpret. The algorithm will happily generate geometry that is theoretically optimal but physically impossible to manufacture or assemble. Learning to read those outputs critically — and know when to override them — is itself a core skill.

The project also introduced me to the concept of the Pareto front in multi-objective optimization: understanding that there's rarely one "best" design, but rather a family of tradeoffs between competing goals like stiffness, mass, and cost.
